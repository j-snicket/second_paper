\begin{abstract}
The complex relationship between galaxies and diffuse gas at large scales depends strongly on astrophysical feedback and cosmology. The dispersion of Fast Radio Bursts (FRBs) is a column of cosmic baryon density and is dominated by the intergalactic medium, which is not necessarily captured by traditional halo model prescriptions. We introduce a diffusion model to reconstruct 3D gas density fields from observed galaxy surveys, including redshift space distortions. The probabilistic model is trained on the CAMELS50 simulation suite (IllustrisTNG) and learns to predict the posterior distribution of gas fields conditioned on stellar mass-weighted galaxy density, cosmological parameters, and the baryon suppression powerspectrum. We validate the model on test boxes by showing agreement between predicted and true dispersion measure across individual lines of sight and the overall dispersion measure distribution.
The model generalizes to a box size $\sim\,4^3$ times larger than the training boxes and can be used to reconstruct the line of sight to cosmological FRBs. The model's 3D gas density predictions vary with input power spectrum and feedback scenarios, opening a new inference path to constrain feedback models from FRBs and other probes of the cosmic baryons. 
\end{abstract}

Most of the baryonic matter in the Universe is thought to reside in the intergalactic medium (IGM) and circumgalactic medium (CGM). This diffuse gas is heated, blown out, and redistributed by stellar and AGN feedback processes. Because it is so diffuse, it is extremely difficult to observe directly, and as a result, the 3D distribution of cosmic baryons remains poorly constrained. A better map of this gas would deepen our understanding of how feedback operates across cosmic time, which is an open questions in galaxy formation. It would also allow us to better interpret the dispersion measures of Fast Radio Bursts, and determine what fraction of each FRB's DM originates from the IGM, the CGM of intervening halos, or the host galaxy.

At the same time, we are entering an era of unprecedented galaxy survey data. The Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument (DESI) has completed its 5-year survey, obtaining spectroscopic redshifts of 50 million galaxies. Upcoming surveys like Rubin/LSST will detect billions more. Galaxies trace the underlying matter distribution on large scales, but their relationship to the diffuse cosmic baryons is less well understood because this gas is difficult to observe. A reconstruction algorithm that maps galaxy surveys directly to the 3D gas distribution would unlock the scientific potential of this data, enabling us to rule out feedback models, interpret FRB sightlines, and map the cosmic baryon distribution.

Fast Radio Bursts (FRBs) are bright, millisecond long radio transients originating from galaxies across the Universe. As the burst propagates toward us, its radio signal interacts with the ionized gas it encounters along the way and higher frequencies arrive earlier than lower frequencies, an effect known as dispersion. The magnitude of this delay, the Dispersion Measure (DM), is directly proportional to the integrated free electron column density along the line of sight. In its journey to Earth, an FRB signal accumulates DM contributions from the gas in its host galaxy, the intergalactic medium, the circumgalactic medium of intervening halos, and the Milky Way. FRBs are therefore a uniquely powerful probe of diffuse ionized gas that is otherwise extremely difficult to detect. However, the DM alone tells us only the total amount of gas along a given sightline and not where that gas came from. Knowledge of the 3D gas distribution along the line of sight can help us distinguish the DM contributions of the IGM, the CGM of intervening halos, and the host galaxy.  

Recent advances in machine learning have dramatically improved our ability to reconstruct 3D cosmic fields from galaxy observations. Combined with  
FRB dispersion measures, these reconstructions allow us to constrain where the baryons are and how they got there, helping us set limits on cosmological and astrophysical parameters (\citep{Khrykin_2024,kglee23,simha2020,simha2023}). 
In this work, we adapt and optimize an existing diffusion based reconstruction framework for 3D gas field reconstruction, tailored  for FRB science. By conditioning the model on the baryon suppression spectrum   we take a step toward using gas reconstructions to discriminate between feedback models, opening a new path for connecting FRB observations to the physics of galaxy formation.

This paper is organized as follows: Section~\ref{sec:methods} describes the simulation data, preprocessing pipeline, and model architecture; Section~\ref{sec:results} presents validation results on held-out CAMELS50 test boxes and generalization to TNG300; Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses implications for FRB science and limitations; and Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} summarizes our conclusions and future directions.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

We train a conditional flow matching model to reconstruct the 3D gas density field from the observed galaxy density field, conditioned on cosmological parameters and the baryon suppression spectrum. We use the CAMELS simulation suite (IllustrisTNG), which provides 1000 simulation boxes each with a volume of $(50\,h^{-1}\,\mathrm{Mpc})^3$ discretized into $128^3$ voxels. Each box contains the 3D stellar mass-weighted galaxy density field, the 3D gas density field, and 36 cosmological and astronomical parameters.

We preprocess the galaxy field by applying a minimum stellar mass threshold $M_{\star} < 10^9\,M_\odot$, populating a $128^3$ voxel grid using Cloud-In-Cell (CIC) interpolation weighted by $\log_{10}(M_{\star})$, computing $\log_{10}$ of the resulting density field, and normalizing by the CAMELS50 training statistics: $\tilde{f}_{\mathrm{gal}} = (\log_{10}(f_{\mathrm{gal}}) - \mu)/\sigma$, where $\mu = -5.94$ and $\sigma = 0.586$.

The gas density field is preprocessed by first applying a $\log_{10}$ transformation (with a small offset of $10^{-6}$ to avoid $\log(0)$), then standardizing to zero mean and unit variance using statistics computed from the training data ($\mu = 9.5$, $\sigma = 0.56$ for the $128^3$ resolution).

\textbf{MISSING: details of how the suppression spectrum is computed exactly. }For each training box, we compute the baryon suppression spectrum $sP(k) = P_\mathrm{hydro}(k)/P_\mathrm{DM}(k)$, the ratio of the baryonic matter power spectrum to the total matter power spectrum. This ratio encodes the combined signature of cosmological parameters (which determine baryon abundance) and feedback strength (which determines baryon redistribution), providing a more sensitive conditioning signal than individual feedback parameters alone.

We use a conditional flow matching model to learn the posterior $p_\theta(n_e(\mathbf{x}) \mid \delta_g(\mathbf{x}), \mathbf{\Omega}, sP(k))$. Flow matching learns to transport samples from a noise distribution $x_0 \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf{I})$ to the target data distribution $x_1$ along a linear interpolation path $x_t = (1-t)x_0 + t x_1$, $t \in [0,1]$, by minimizing the mean-squared error between the predicted and true velocity fields $u_t = x_1 - x_0$. At inference time, the learned velocity field is integrated using a neural ODE solver with 50 Euler steps. The backbone is a 3D conditional U-Net operating on $128^3$ voxel grids, with three encoder/decoder levels at channel dimensions $[32, 64, 64]$, $3\times3\times3$ circular-padded convolutions, group normalization, and SiLU activations. The galaxy field is concatenated channel-wise with the target field; cosmological parameters and $sP(k)$ are embedded via sinusoidal positional encodings and broadcast to all U-Net blocks. The model is trained with AdamW at a learning rate of $10^{-4}$, effective batch size 16 (4 gradient accumulation steps), $\ell_2$ gradient clipping at 1.0, and 16-bit mixed precision for 30,000 gradient steps. We use 980 boxes for training, 10 for validation, and 10 for testing.

To test generalization, we apply the trained model to TNG300, a simulation four times larger than the training boxes in volume $(302\,h^{-1}\,\mathrm{Mpc})^3$. We preprocess the TNG300 galaxy field using the same pipeline as above, rescaling galaxy positions by a factor of $50/35$ to match the CAMELS50 voxel resolution, and apply the same normalization statistics from training.

All simulations are evaluated at redshift $z = 0$. Dispersion measures are computed by summing the reconstructed gas density field along the line of sight, consistent with the definition of DM as the integral of electron column density.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
Figure~\ref{fig:input_output} shows the model's reconstruction quality on a CAMELS50 test box. Although the model works in 3D density fields, , to connect to FRB science, we sum the reconstructed gas along the line of sight to produce 2D dispersion measure maps. The reconstructed DM map  recovers the large scale structure of filaments, nodes, and voids present in the True DM map. To quantify this, the bottom panels show a scatter plot of the posterior mean DM against the true DM across all lines of sight in the box, and a histogram comparing the full DM distributions. The scatter is tightly clustered around the 1:1 line with $\sigma = 0.124$(xxx keep updating), and the reconstructed DM distribution closely matches the true distribution. The model accurately reconstructs both individual sightlines and the overall statistics of the gas field.

To validate the model's predictions, figure \ref{fig:patch_hist_box9} shows 150 independent samples drawn from the model at three representative locations: a void, a filament, and a dense node. In all three environments, the model's samples cluster around the ground truth value. The model provides a posterior distribution rather than a point estimate, with the true value consistently positioned within the predicted distribution. This demonstrates that the model has learned realistic uncertainties: the posterior is neither overconfident (exhibiting a sharp peak that could miss ground truth) nor uninformative (spanning an unreasonably wide range).
\\
To assess the model's ability to preserve the relative ordering of DM values, we computed the rank correlation between true and reconstructed dispersion measures. Figure \ref{fig:rank} shows a 2D histogram comparing the rank of true DM values to the rank of reconstructed DM values across all lines of sight in a test box. High density concentrated along the diagonal (white dashed line) indicates that the model accurately preserves the relative ordering of DM values from lowest to highest. This demonstrates that even where absolute DM predictions may deviate from the truth, the model correctly identifies which regions have more or less gas than others. This rank based metric confirms that the model captures the amplitude distribution and relative structure of the cosmic web.\\

Figure~\ref{fig:pk} explores how the model responds to different feedback scenarios. We fed the same galaxy field into the model three times, each with a different suppression spectrum: high, correct, and low feedback. With high feedback, the model produces a smoother, more diffuse gas, as expected when strong feedback has blown gas out of dense structures into the voids. With the correct spectrum matching the test box, the reconstruction matches the true gas distribution. With low feedback, the model produces a more concentrated distribution with tighter filaments and denser nodes. 

To test whether the model generalizes beyond the training distribution, we applied it to TNG300, a simulation box four times larger than the CAMELS50 training data (302 vs 50 $h^{-1}$ Mpc on a side). We rescaled the TNG300 data using the same preprocessing pipeline as the training data. Figure \ref{fig:tng300} shows that the model successfully reconstructs the DM distribution across the larger box. The reconstructed DM histogram closely matches the true distribution, which demonstrates that the model learned generalizable physics rather than memorizing training patterns, and can scale to significantly larger volumes than those used for training. Although the model shows a systematic underprediction of approximately 0.1 in $\log_{10}(\mathrm{DM})$ on average which could be due to the normalization process (we normalize using CAMELS50 mean/std, but TNG300 might have different gas statistics. Ask Aizhan.)
As a way to show how the model can be used to study individual FRB lines of sight, we stitched together 10 CAMELS50 boxes to creat a line of sight that extends to $\sim500\,h^{-1}\,\mathrm{Mpc}$. Figure \ref{fig:cigar} shows the reconstructed vs the true gas of the test boxes. So, the model can be generalized to large boxes like we showed in \ref{fig:tng300}, but we can also stitch multiple boxes together to create a long line of sight and that would still be informative.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
In generalization of our model to TNG 300, and also with the CAMELS test boxes, the distribution of dispersion measure in the reconstruction has a systematic offset from that of the true distribution (Figure \ref{fig:tng300}). At the same time, the rank correlation in Figure \ref{fig:rank} demonstrates that the relative ordering of the DM values is accurate with a Spearman rank correlation of 0.8517 xxx. This validates that the relative gas distribution in the reconstruction is trustworthy. Therefore, for the FRB science applications, this is not problematic because given the known value of $\Omega_b$ from cosmology and observed DM of an FRB, we can calibrate out the offset and extract accurate absolute DM. 

Limitations: \\
1. the minimum stellar mass threshold used to define the galaxy sample is not calibrated to a specific observational survey. The reconstruction is sensitive to this choice as shown in figure xxx.\\
2. the model has the same systematic assumptions and biases present in the CAMELS IllustrisTNG simulations. If the real Universe differs from these assumptions, the reconstructions will be biased. \\
3. The model is conditioned on cosmological parameters assumed to be known exactly, whereas in practice they carry observational uncertainties. \\



\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusion}
We have introduced a diffusion-based model for reconstructing the 3D distribution of cosmic baryons from galaxy survey data, conditioned on cosmological parameters and the baryon suppression spectrum. Trained on the CAMELS50 simulation suite, the model learns to predict the posterior distribution of gas density fields given stellar mass-weighted galaxy density. We validated the model on test boxes, demonstrating accurate reconstruction of both the spatial structure and statistical distribution of the gas field, as measured by dispersion measure (DM) along lines of sight. The model generalizes to larger box sizes, successfully reconstructing DM distributions in TNG300. By conditioning on the baryon suppression spectrum, the model captures the integrated effects of feedback processes, allowing for future applications in constraining feedback models from FRB observations. This framework opens a new avenue for mapping the cosmic baryons and leveraging FRBs as probes of large-scale structure and galaxy evolution.

Future work will explore the sensitivity of reconstructions to different galaxy selection functions, training on multiple simulation suites to mitigate model dependence, and incorporating cosmological parameter uncertainties into the conditioning framework. Despite these caveats, our results demonstrate that diffusion models can effectively reconstruct the 3D distribution of cosmic baryons from galaxy observations, providing a powerful new tool for interpreting FRB measurements and probing the cosmic web.
