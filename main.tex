\begin{abstract}
The complex relationship between galaxies and diffuse gas at large scales depends strongly on astrophysical feedback and cosmology. The dispersion of Fast Radio Bursts (FRBs) is a column of cosmic baryon density and is dominated by the intergalactic medium, which is not necessarily captured by traditional halo model prescriptions. We introduce a diffusion model to reconstruct 3D gas density fields from observed galaxy surveys, including redshift space distortions. The probabilistic model is trained on the CAMELS50 simulation suite (IllustrisTNG) and learns to predict the posterior distribution of gas fields conditioned on stellar mass-weighted galaxy density, cosmological parameters, and the baryon suppression powerspectrum. We validate the model on test boxes by showing agreement between predicted and true dispersion measure across individual lines of sight and the overall dispersion measure distribution.
The model generalizes to a box size $\sim\,4^3$ times larger than the training boxes and can be used to reconstruct the line of sight to cosmological FRBs. The model's 3D gas density predictions vary with input power spectrum and feedback scenarios, opening a new inference path to constrain feedback models from FRBs and other probes of the cosmic baryons. 
\end{abstract}

% What is an FRB and why do astronomers care about it?
FRBs are bright, short-duration (ms), transient, radio signals coming from all over the universe from other galaxies. The radio signal of the burst interacts with the ionized diffuse gas it encounters on its path, and higher frequencies arrive later than lower frequencies. The measurement of this delay, called the dispersion measure, can tell us how much ionized gas was along the line of sight of the FRB. In its path, the FRB interacts with gas in its host galaxy, the intergalactic medium, circumgalactic medium of other galaxies it passes by, and the gas in the Milky Way. By measuring the dispersion measure, we can determine the total sum of all these matters. This is important because some of the matter in the universe, like the intergalactic medium, is very hard to detect by other methods given that it is diffuse and hot. But with FRBs, we can prove this diffuse matter and measure the quantity of it. 

% Why do we need to know what's in the gas between galaxies?

Most of the matter in the universe is between galaxies, in the intergalactic medium. Having a reconstruction of the gas between galaxies is mapping the "missing baryons." Understanding how that matter is structured will help us understand how supernova and AGN feedback work. Having a good reconstruction of the gas between galaxies will help us discriminate against unphysical feedback models. 

% How do we currently try to reconstruct the gas between galaxies?

We know the distribution of galaxies in the universe through observations and surveys, be it noisy or with biased selection functions. But any distribution of gas between galaxies that we have right now is based on simulations. There is no real observational input. And the connection between observed matter (galaxies) and gas is not straightforward. Luckily, there has been significant progress in recent years in machine learning methods. Using an existing algorithm for 3D reconstruction and optimizing it for FRBs, we are introducing a framework where inputting the galaxy density field and stellar mass, we can produce a 3D map of the hidden gas between galaxies. 
\\
\\
3D reconstruction of the cosmic web is so helpful when using FRBs as probes for the large scale structure. Luckily, due to advances in machine learning, the models for 3D reconstruction have gotten so much better. We are optimizing an existing algorithm to use it for 3D gas reconstruction and tailoring it to FRB studies. By training the model in a feedback conditional way, we will one day be able to use reconstruction of the gas to discriminate against unfavorable feedback parameters.

To test if our model could be generalized to bigger box sizes, we ran inference on TNG boxes. Our model had trained on the TNG suite in the CAMELS simulation, so it should work equally as well on a TNG input. The box size that our model trained on was 50 mpc $^3$, but we ran inference on boxes as large as 302 mpc$^3$ and recovered good reconstructions. 
\\
The dispersion measure (DM) is a measure of the total amount of gas along the line of sight toward a source. It is defined as the integral of electron density along the path:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{DM} = \int_0^d n_e(l)\,dl
\end{equation}
where $n_e(l)$ is the electron density at distance $l$ along the line of sight. In practice, DM tells us how much ionized gas the radio signal passed through. In our simulations, we compute DM by summing the gas density along one spatial direction (the line of sight), which produces a 2D map where each pixel value represents the total gas column in that direction. This makes DM a key observable: FRBs measure DM directly, and our reconstructed gas fields allow us to predict what DM values should be for any location in the universe.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}
In this work we use 3D maps from CAMELS50 to model the connection between galaxy field and gas. We use the IllustrisTNG suite. CAMELS simulations have a box volume of $(50\,h^{-1}\,\mathrm{Mpc})^3$. We used 1000 simulation boxes with dimensions $50 \times 50 \times 50\,(\,h^{-1}\,\mathrm{Mpc})^3$ that included the parameters for that box, 3D galaxy density weighted by stellar mass, and gas 3D density. Each box is 128 x 128 x 128 pixels. 

Since TNG50 has a box size of $(35\,h^{-1}\,\mathrm{Mpc})^3$, which differs from the CAMELS50 training data at $(50\,h^{-1}\,\mathrm{Mpc})^3$, we needed to rescale the TNG50 data to match the model's training distribution. The volume difference results in different pixel sizes and galaxy number densities per voxel.
While our models demonstrate sensitivity to changes in cosmological parameters such as $\Omega_b$, $H_0$, $\Omega_m$, $\sigma_8$, and $n_s$, they show minimal sensitivity to parameters controlling AGN and supernova feedback (e.g., \texttt{BlackHoleRadiativeEfficiency}, \texttt{BlackHoleAccretionFactor}). When we vary these feedback parameters between their minimum and maximum values in the training set, the reconstructed gas distributions remain essentially unchanged. This lack of sensitivity is problematic because feedback fundamentally shapes the gas distribution: maximal feedback should produce smoother reconstructions (as gas is expelled from filaments and nodes into voids), and broader dispersion measure distributions (due to fewer extreme DM values). To capture these feedback driven changes, we needed a more integrated tracer that captures the cumulative effect of all feedback processes rather than individual parameters.

To address this, we trained a new model conditioned on the baryon suppression spectrum as opposed to individual feedback parameters. We computed the baryon suppression spectrum for each training box by taking the ratio of the baryonic matter power spectrum to the total matter (baryonic + dark matter) power spectrum.  This ratio quantifies the imprint of feedback on structure formation: regions where feedback has been efficient show suppressed power (low ratios), while regions less affected by feedback trace the dark matter more closely (high ratios). The suppression spectrum therefore encodes the combined signature of cosmological parameters (which determine bar\textbf{}yon abundance) and feedback strength (which determines baryon redistribution). By conditioning the diffusion model on both cosmological parameters and the suppression spectrum, we created a framework sensitive to the integrated feedback signature. Now when we vary the suppression spectrum to represent low or high feedback scenarios, the model produces corresponding changes in the reconstructed gas distributions as demonstrated in figures (\ref{fig:placeholder_pk} and \ref{fig:placeholder_pk_hist}).

We preprocessed the TNG50 data as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Rescaled all galaxy positions by a factor of $\frac{50}{35}$ to map them into the CAMELS50 box size.
    \item Created a $128^3$ voxel grid spanning the rescaled $50\,h^{-1}\,\mathrm{Mpc}$ box.
    \item Applied a minimum stellar mass threshold, removing all galaxies with $M_{\star} < M_{\star,\mathrm{min}}$. The minimum stellar mass was variable and we tried having different cutoffs to see if our results would change. We tried these values: 1e9, 1e10 and 1e11. 
    \item Populated the grid using Cloud-In-Cell (CIC) interpolation, weighting each galaxy by $\log_{10}(M_{\star})$, where $M_{\star}$ is stellar mass.
    \item Computed $\log_{10}$ of the resulting density field.
    \item Normalized using the mean and standard deviation from CAMELS50 training: 
    $\tilde{f}_{\mathrm{gal}} = \frac{\log_{10}(f_{\mathrm{gal}}) - \mu}{\sigma}$, 
    where $\mu = \text{-5.94}$ and $\sigma = \text{0.586}$.
\end{enumerate}

What matters in FRB science is the accuracy of the dispersion measure from a sightline (a single pixel). In figure \ref{fig:placeholder} the scatter plot compares the reconstruction DM and true DM across all sightlines (pixels) in a CAMELS test box. The plot shows a tight scatter around a 1:1 line with $\sigma = 0.124$(xxx keep updating). The model does better in lower DM higher DMs as the scatter broadens with higher DMs. The histogram of the same figure demonstrates that the model can reproduce the distribution of DMs accurately, not just the individual sightlines. which means the model knows how to map between galaxies (input) and gas (output). 

To validate the model's predictions, we examined DM samples at individual pixels across different cosmic environments. Figure \ref{fig:patch_hist_box9} shows 150 independent samples drawn from the model at three representative locations: a void, a filament, and a dense node. In all three environments, the model's samples cluster tightly around the ground truth value. This is important because instead of giving a single point estimate like "DM = 1000", the model provides a distribution: "DM is likely between X and Y." The true value sits in the middle of this distribution which shows the model has learned realistic uncertainty. It's not overconfident (which would give you a sharp spike that might miss the truth) and it's not useless (which would give you a huge spread).
\\
To assess the model's ability to preserve the relative ordering of DM values, we computed the rank correlation between true and reconstructed dispersion measures. Figure \ref{fig:rank} shows a 2D histogram comparing the rank of true DM values to the rank of reconstructed DM values across all lines of sight in a test box. High density concentrated along the diagonal (white dashed line) indicates that the model accurately preserves the relative ordering of DM values from lowest to highest. This demonstrates that even where absolute DM predictions may deviate from the truth, the model correctly identifies which regions have more or less gas than others. This rank-based metric is particularly robust to systematic biases and confirms that the model captures the amplitude distribution and relative structure of the cosmic web.\\
To test whether the model generalizes beyond the training distribution, we applied it to TNG300, a simulation box six times larger than the CAMELS50 training data (302 vs 50 $h^{-1}$ Mpc on a side). We rescaled the TNG300 data using the same preprocessing pipeline as the training data. Figure \ref{fig:tng300_generalization} shows that the model successfully reconstructs the DM distribution across the larger box. The reconstructed DM histogram closely matches the true distribution, which demonstrates that the model learned generalizable physics rather than memorizing training patterns, and can scale to significantly larger volumes than those used for training. \section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
In generalization of our model to TNG 300, and also in general with the test boxes, the distribution of dispersion measure in the reconstruction has a systematic offset from that of the true distribution (Figure \ref{fig:tng300}). At the same time, the rank correlation in Figure \ref{fig:rank} demonstrates that the relative ordering of the DM values is accurate with a Spearman rank correlation of 0.8517 xxx. This validates that the relative gas distribution in the reconstruction is trustworthy. Therefore, for the FRB science applications, this is not problematic because given the known value of $\Omega_b$ from cosmology and observed DM of an FRB, we can calibrate out the offset and extract accurate absolute DM.
